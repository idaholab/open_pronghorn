Free jet flows have been extensively examined through both experimental and numerical studies, especially within the realms of aeronautics, as they are generated by the exhaust flow of aircraft engines or used for blade cooling in turbomachinery. The dynamics of jet flows are very complicated and significantly affected by factors such as the Reynolds number, nozzle diameter, fluid properties, and flow conditions. Shear flow is the dominant phenomena. The prediction of free shear flows is highly sensitive to the accurate modeling of Reynolds stress, turbulent kinetic energy, and its dissipation rate. The absence of nearwall turbulence complexity in free jet flow allows precise evaluation of a turbulence model’s accuracy in predicting the general flow field without the interference of wall turbulence. This validation case uses data published in the experiment presented in Turutoglu et. all work [!cite](turutoglu2024calibration). 

## Problem Description

In this experiment a free jet flows into a large tank filled with still air. Experimental setup consists of a jet flow unit that generates a round free jet discharging from a nozzle, and an air supply system. Compressed air used to generate jet flow is stored in a tank. Flow is obtained by passing the pressurized air through a pressure regulator and then directing into a stagnation chamber. After the stagnation chamber, flow is discharged into the atmosphere passing through a nozzle. The exit diameter of the nozzle is 12 mm. Contraction ratio of the jet nozzle is 4. Jet velocity is controlled by adjusting the pressure inside the stagnation chamber with a close loop algorithm in jet flow unit system. Velocity and turbulence profiles are measured by moving the probe in the radial direction. In addition, jet centerline velocities and turbulence intensities are measured along the jet centerline to examine the change of the speed and turbulence intensity on the jet axis with increasing distance from the jet exit plane.

## `OpenPronghorn` Model

In the geometry created for the CFD analyses, the jet outlet diameter D was determined as 10 mm matching the value in the source document. Aditionally, Faghani et al. [!cite](faghani2011effect) mentioned that a uniform inlet velocity profile shows better agreement with experimental data from similar studies on jet flow. The turbulence intensity at the inlet surface was set to 5% to align with the experimental data. This validation case demonstrates the simulation of a round free jet in a still tank using `OpenPronghorn` K-Epsilon model. Enabling the realizable two layer variation, and Wolfstein two-layer model. The mesh is generated using the MOOSE mesh generator system

## Discussion of results

In both plots the red line presents the `OpenPronghorn` results. IP-RSM stands for isotropization by production reynolds stress model (IP-RMS), and it is the turbulence model chosen by Turutoglu et. all in their numerical simulation. For the axial-velocity prediction the `OpenPronghorn` simulation yields better results than both the default and calibrated IP-RSM even though K-Epsilon is considered a lower fidelity turbulence model. For the halfwidth prediction, the `OpenPronghorn` simulation sits between the two IP-RMS versions in terms of agreement with the experimental data.
